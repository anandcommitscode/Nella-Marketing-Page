Part 1: Voice and pattern library
How nella sounds
She writes the way she would speak to a close friend over a glass of wine. Confident, warm, specific, never showing off. The site reads like one woman talking to another. Lowercase mental register, sentence case as default, full stops doing the work that em dashes used to do.
Words nella uses
Track, log, check, see, remember, know, sort, fix, stop, start, ready, due, overdue, paused, on, off, today, tomorrow, every, all, your, hers, mine. Plain English nouns: routine, treatment, product, clinic, score, history, money, spend, shelf. Comfortable specifics: retinol, microneedling, botox, vitamin C, brow lamination, gel manicure.
Words nella does not use
Unlock, empower, transform, journey, elevate, level up, discover (as a verb), seamless, intuitive (as a marketing word), holistic, curated, bespoke, premium, leverage, harness, dive in, get started, take control, optimise (as a marketing word), revolutionary, game-changer, ultimate, smart (overused, but locked as the feature name "smart reminders"), personalised (clinical, but locked as the feature name "personalised intelligence cards"), wellness (when she means beauty). On the question of "we": nella speaks as nella across the proposition pages, the stories, and the product pages. The company behind her (mynella Ltd) speaks where a legal or organisational commitment is being made (the privacy principles, the terms, the press kit ownership, the legal pages). Everywhere else, "we" gives way to the brand voice: "every message gets read" rather than "we read every message."
Sentence rules
Sentences are short. Three lines on a phone is the upper bound, two is better. Paragraphs are two to four sentences. If a paragraph runs longer, it gets split. Lists are used when items are genuinely a list. Prose is used when items belong together as a thought.
Capitalisation rules
The brand is lowercase always: nella, nella Core, nella Pro. Headings are sentence case. Buttons are sentence case. The only places title case appears are external brand names referenced verbatim (App Store, Apple, Reddit, Instagram, TikTok).
How nella names a benefit
Plain noun. Tracking. Routines. Aftercare. Insights. No adjective, no verb, no possessive.
How nella writes a CTA
The CTA is a clear action with no marketing wrapper. Pre-launch state: "join the waitlist." Post-launch state: "get nella." Secondary actions follow the same logic: "see pricing," "read the article," "open in the App Store."
How nella writes a hero
Hero headline is short. Six to ten words. Subheading is one sentence. CTA is one button. Supporting visual is the app or the score. The whole block fits above the fold on a 390 pixel viewport.
How nella opens a page
A direct statement of what the page is about, in her voice. Never a question to the reader as the first line. Never "welcome to." Never throat-clearing.
Phase-aware CTA convention
Every CTA in this document carries two states:
Pre-launch: "join the waitlist" → /waitlist
Post-launch: "get nella" → /download
The CMS phase flag switches them. Where the CTA appears as a secondary button or text link, the same logic applies (e.g. "join the waitlist" becomes "download nella").

Lead paragraph:
the short answer is yes, with the right schedule. the long answer involves a small amount of chemistry, a recurring myth, and the schedule most dermatologists actually recommend. here is the version you can use tonight.
H2: the myth.
Body:
the original concern, which is still repeated everywhere from tiktok to product packaging, is that vitamin C and retinol cancel each other out. the science behind that claim is partly accurate and largely outdated.
the accurate bit: vitamin C is most stable and most effective at a low pH, around 3 to 3.5. retinol is most effective at a higher pH, around 5.5 to 6. used in the same routine, back to back, the pH conflict can reduce the effectiveness of one or both.
the outdated bit: modern formulations have largely solved this. most well-formulated vitamin C serums use ascorbic acid in a form that is stable across a wider pH range. most well-formulated retinols are buffered to work alongside other actives. the chemistry that made the original advice cautious has moved on.
H2: the reality.
Body:
for most women, using vitamin C in the morning and retinol at night is the cleanest, most effective schedule. it sidesteps the pH question entirely because the two products never meet on your skin. it also matches the way these ingredients actually work: vitamin C is an antioxidant that protects against daytime UV and pollution damage, retinol is a cell-turnover ingredient that does its work overnight.
if you have been told you cannot use both, you can. you just need the schedule.
H2: the schedule that works for most skin.
Body lead-in:
a simple template, three frequencies, depending on how reactive your skin is.
H3: if your skin tolerates actives well.
vitamin C every morning. retinol three to five nights a week. on the off-nights, hydrating or barrier-supporting actives like niacinamide or peptides.
H3: if your skin is on the sensitive side.
vitamin C every other morning. retinol two nights a week. always with a moisturiser layered on top of the retinol, and SPF religiously every morning.
H3: if you are new to either.
introduce one at a time. start with vitamin C in the AM for two weeks. if your skin is happy, introduce retinol once a week in the PM. build up gradually over six weeks to two or three nights.
H2: the rules underneath the schedule.
Body lead-in:
four rules that make the schedule work, regardless of which version of it you are on.
H3: rule one: SPF in the morning, every morning.
retinol and vitamin C both increase sun sensitivity. SPF 30 minimum, SPF 50 if you can. without it, you are undoing the work of both ingredients.
H3: rule two: do not layer retinol with AHA or BHA on the same night.
retinol and exfoliating acids are both active. used together, they can overwhelm the skin barrier. AHA on one PM, retinol on another. never both at once.
H3: rule three: moisturise on retinol nights.
retinol can be drying. a good moisturiser layered over the top reduces irritation without reducing effectiveness.
H3: rule four: wait for the burn-in.
retinol takes six to twelve weeks to show its full effect. vitamin C takes about four to six. if you have just started, do not judge either product before three months.

H2: the order is the easy part.
Body:
the principle takes one line: thinnest to thickest, water-based before oil-based, actives between cleanser and moisturiser, SPF last in the morning. it is genuinely that simple. if your routine is in roughly the right order, the order is not the problem.
what tends to be the problem is one or more of four quieter things. they look like consistency issues, ingredient choices, or "my skin is just like this." they are not. they are the four things that break a routine without ever announcing themselves.
H2: the four things that break a routine.
H3: one: your actives are running at the wrong frequency.
retinol does its work at three to five nights a week, consistently, over six to twelve weeks. most routines aim for three and land at two. vitamin C is similar in the morning: daily is the brief, four times a week is what actually happens. the dose-response curve for both ingredients is steep at the lower end, which means the difference between two nights of retinol and four is much larger than the difference between four and six. half the people who think retinol is not working for them are simply not using it often enough for long enough.
H3: two: too many actives, too close together.
retinol on monday, AHA on tuesday, BHA on wednesday, vitamin C every morning. the routine looks rigorous. the skin barrier reads it as constant low-grade assault. the result is the same skin that the actives are meant to be improving, only now it is also red around the nose and tight along the jaw. one active per evening, with at least one rest night between exfoliating acids and retinol, is almost always more effective than three.
H3: three: the hydration step quietly disappears.
actives are exciting. SPF is non-negotiable. moisturiser and hydrating serum are the bit in the middle that gets skipped on a tuesday night because the actives feel like the real work. they are not. a routine without proper hydration is a routine where every active is being asked to do its job on a skin barrier that is not equipped to receive it. hyaluronic acid on damp skin, then moisturiser, in both the AM and the PM. it is not the glamorous part of the routine. it is the part that decides whether the rest of it lands.
H3: four: SPF that is not really happening.
SPF every morning. SPF 30 minimum, SPF 50 if you can. applied all over the face and neck. reapplied if you are outside for more than a couple of hours. most routines tick the first box, sometimes the second, almost never the third or fourth. UV is the single largest factor in how fast collagen breaks down, how persistent pigmentation becomes, and how visible the work of your retinol ever gets. a perfect PM routine with a sloppy AM SPF is a routine running net negative. this is the unflattering truth about most plateaus.
H2: the order, for reference.
Body lead-in:
if you do want the order, plainly stated, this is it. AM first, then PM. brief because the order is the easy part.
H3: AM, in order.
cleanser. (optional toner.) vitamin C serum. hydrating serum. moisturiser. SPF. (makeup, if you wear it.)
H3: PM, in order.
makeup removal, if needed. cleanser. (optional toner.) one active (retinol on retinol nights, an exfoliating acid on those nights, never both). hydrating serum. moisturiser. (optional face oil or sleep mask.)
H2: the rules that actually matter.
Body, four short blocks:
wait between actives. let each one absorb. it makes a real difference.
one active per session. retinol and AHA on the same night is too much for most skin. niacinamide is the exception, it sits comfortably alongside almost everything.
water-based before oil-based. a serum will not penetrate through an oil. a hyaluronic acid will not work if you have already applied moisturiser.
SPF, every morning. even in winter. even if you work indoors. even if it is grey.
H2: when something changes.
Body, one paragraph:
booked a treatment, the order changes. microneedling means no retinol for seven days, no vitamin C for three, no acids for at least a week. peels are longer, depending on grade. botox does not change the routine. (full pause logic: /intelligence/ingredient-conflicts.)
H2: how nella handles this.
Body, two paragraphs:
in nella, your routine is held in the right order automatically and scheduled at the right frequency for each active. retinol on the nights it should be, vitamin C every morning, hydration in the step where it gets remembered, SPF as the final tick of the morning routine. the order is not the thing you have to hold in your head.
what nella also holds is the four breaks. how consistently you have actually completed your routine over the last fourteen days. how often your hydration step is missed. whether your SPF is being logged every morning or only most mornings. those four inputs do not just sit in your dashboard, they feed your score. so when your skin is not moving the way you expected, you can see why. and fix the actual thing, not the order.

Article
H1: the wedding beauty timeline: what to book, when, and what to leave alone.
Lead paragraph:
a wedding is the one occasion where the timing of your beauty matters more than the spend. start the right things early and they work. start them late and they backfire. this is the twelve-month plan in plain english, from the early baseline work down to the morning of, with the bit at the end that most timelines forget to mention: what not to do in the final four weeks.
H2: twelve to nine months out.
Body:
the long game work goes here. this is the only window where you have time for things that compound: a serious skincare routine that calibrates over three months, body work that requires a full course, dental whitening if you are doing it, and any consultation appointments with the people you trust.
book your skin consultation now. start a properly built routine, with retinol and vitamin C scheduled at sustainable frequencies. if you are considering injectables or skin treatments you have never had before, this is when you trial them. you want to see how your face responds with a year of margin, not a month. trial profhilo, trial polynucleotides, trial a new aesthetician. start your laser hair removal course, which needs six to eight sessions across as many months. start any orthodontic or dental work that requires runway.
H2: nine to six months out.
Body:
this is the locking-in window. the treatments you trialled and liked move into a regular cadence. brows go on a six-week cycle so they peak around the dress fitting. facials settle into a four to six-week rhythm. hair colour finds the tone you want, which sometimes takes two or three sittings to get right.
if you are doing botox for the wedding, you do not need to book it now. but if it is new to you, your trial appointment goes here. you want to see the effect at full peak (around week two) and the wear-off pattern (around week ten) before you decide on a wedding-day cadence. the same goes for any filler. trial it, see how you feel about it for the full duration, then decide whether to repeat closer to the day.
H2: six to three months out.
Body:
this is the consolidation window. your routine is doing its work. your treatments are on cycle. the photographer is booked, the venue is paid for, and your skin is roughly where you want it to be by the wedding.
this is also the right window for any course-based treatments that finish before the final month. a course of three microneedling sessions, spaced four to six weeks apart, starts here. a course of skin boosters or polynucleotides starts here. you want the last session of any course to land at least six weeks before the wedding, not the week before.
your hair gets a proper trial with whoever is doing it on the day, with the dress photographs or fabric swatches. so does your makeup. the bridal trial does not have to be expensive. it has to be definitive.
H2: three to one month out.
Body:
the final treatment window for anything that comes with downtime, swelling, or unpredictability. botox lands here, three to four weeks out, so the result is fully settled by the day. botox takes about two weeks to reach full effect, which gives you a two-week buffer for any review or small adjustment your practitioner suggests. earlier than three weeks risks under-settled results. later than two weeks means walking into the wedding mid-development. four weeks out is the sweet spot.
filler timing depends on the area. lip filler needs at least six weeks, ideally eight, because lips swell and bruise more than other areas, and small bumps can take a full month to settle. cheek, jaw, or chin filler needs six to eight weeks. anything sooner risks visible swelling or bruising on the day.
at four weeks out, the final microneedling, the final peel of any grade, the final deep facial that involves extractions. anything more aggressive than a hydrating treatment stops at this point.
H2: the final month: what to do, what to leave alone
Body:
this is the section most timelines skip. the bit where you do less, not more.
no new actives, no new products, no new clinics. the routine you have been running for six months is the one you finish on. introducing a new serum in the final month is one of the most common ways to walk into the wedding with a reaction you have never seen before.
no aggressive treatments. no microneedling, no peels, no laser, no aggressive extractions. a calm hydrating facial two weeks out is fine. anything more is gambling with downtime you cannot afford. no significant changes to your hair colour in the final four weeks unless it is the same colourist doing the same shade. the touch-up window is the week before, not earlier and not later.
no last-minute injectables. the temptation is real and the regret is consistent. if you missed the six-week window for botox, the answer is to skip it for the wedding and do it the week after, not to squeeze it in at three weeks. the same for filler, even more so.
H2: the final two weeks.
Body:
the final brow shape and tint at twelve days out, so it has settled and softened by the day. if you have lash extensions, the final infill at five to seven days out. if you have lash lifts, the same timing. eyebrow lamination, if you do it, two to three weeks out, never closer.
hair colour in the seven to ten-day window. close enough to look fresh, far enough to look natural rather than just-coloured. a glossing or toner appointment two days before is the optional extra that takes a colour from very good to excellent.
nails in the forty-eight to seventy-two hour window. close enough that they survive the day, far enough that the cuticles have settled.
H2: the day before and the morning of.
Body:
the day before is for the things you would do anyway. your standard PM routine. extra hydration if your skin is feeling tight, nothing new. a moisturising mask if it is in your usual rotation. early to bed and water, the two things every bride knows and half of them skip.
the morning of is for the routine you have been running for six months, exactly as you have been running it. cleanser, the serums you trust, the moisturiser that sits well under makeup, SPF (yes, even with your wedding hair and makeup on top, the photographs are the reason). if you have wedding makeup booked, the artist will work over the top. if you are doing your own, give your moisturiser and SPF five to ten minutes to fully settle before you start.
H2: how nella handles all of this.
Body, two paragraphs:
once you set your wedding date in nella, the timeline above becomes your timeline. the reminders adjust. the treatment cycles recalibrate. your routine knows the wedding is coming and protects against the things that would undermine it. no new actives in the final month. no aggressive treatments inside the four-week window. the final brow appointment timed to land twelve days out, the nails timed to land within seventy-two hours.
what nella also holds is the record. every trial, every clinic, every product, every outcome from the twelve months of preparation. so when your sister is planning hers eighteen months later, you do not have to remember. nella does.

Part 4: Final consistency notes for the developer
A short list of things to check before launch.
Voice consistency. The site is written in one voice. Sentence case headings throughout. Lowercase brand name throughout. No em dashes. No exclamation marks. No marketing verbs.
CTA consistency. Every primary CTA on every page uses the phase-aware string. Every secondary CTA uses the same logic. Where a CTA appears as a text link instead of a button, the verb is consistent ("read more", "see how it works", "see all the features").
Internal linking. Every page links to at least two other pages on the site. Most link to three or four. Internal links use descriptive anchor text, not "click here" or "read more" alone.
Heading hierarchy. Every page has one H1. Every section is an H2. Sub-sections are H3. The hierarchy is never skipped.
Meta titles. Every meta title is under 60 characters. Where possible, the brand name "nella" appears in the title for brand recognition in search.
Meta descriptions. Every meta description is under 155 characters and reads as a natural sentence, not a keyword list.
Alt text. Every image carries descriptive alt text. (Suggestion: alt text written by the developer in line with the image content, not auto-generated. Examples provided in OG image notes.)
Schema. Each page has its schema noted in the SEO block. Implement as JSON-LD in the page head.
Phase toggle. Every CTA, every "join the waitlist" reference, every App Store badge presence is wired to the siteSettings.launchPhase flag in Sanity. Flipping the flag switches the whole site.
